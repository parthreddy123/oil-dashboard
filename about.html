How the Oil Scenario Engine Was Built

A vibe coding journey — from "build me an oil dashboard" to an AI-powered geopolitical intelligence platform

The Prompt That Started It All

It started with a single prompt to Claude Code: "Build me an oil and gas intelligence dashboard. Streamlit. Dark theme. Real data."

No spec. No wireframes. No architecture document. Just a vibe and a direction. The kind of thing that either produces something incredible or collapses under its own ambiguity.

What followed was a multi-week conversation between a human with domain intuition and an AI that could write production code — iterating through failures, pivots, and breakthroughs until the system you see today emerged.

Chapter 1: The Foundation (Days 1-2)

Getting the scaffolding right

Claude set up a Streamlit multi-page app with 8 pages, a SQLite backend in WAL mode, and a dark theme. The initial architecture was clean: scrapers, processing, database, dashboard. Simple separation of concerns.

The EIA API was the first real data source. Brent and WTI crude prices flowed in — 730 daily records stretching back two years. Then product prices: petrol, diesel, ATF, propane. The conversion from USD/gallon to USD/bbl (multiply by 42) seems obvious but getting it wrong would cascade through every calculation downstream.

First win
Real data on screen within hours. Brent price chart rendering in dark theme. The dopamine hit that keeps you going.

Chapter 2: The Indian Basket Problem

Where things got complicated

Indian refiners don't buy Brent. They buy a basket: 72% Dubai/Oman + 28% Brent. This is the PPAC official methodology. But Dubai crude doesn't have a free API. It's not on EIA. It's not on yfinance.

Failure #1 — Dubai crude pricing
First attempt: scrape OilPrice.com. Hit with Cloudflare protection. Second attempt: different User-Agent headers and request patterns. Finally got through, but the data was spotty — sometimes the page structure changed and the scraper broke silently, returning stale prices for days before anyone noticed.

Added yfinance as a backup for Brent (BZ=F futures) since EIA data has a 1-day lag. Now the dashboard showed live-ish prices. The Indian basket formula finally worked, and the GRM calculation could use the right crude cost.

Chapter 3: The GRM Rabbit Hole

Six products, six problems

Gross Refining Margin sounds simple: product revenue minus crude cost. But Indian complex refineries produce 6 products with different yield weights: diesel (42%), petrol (22%), naphtha (12%), ATF (10%), fuel oil (8%), LPG (6%).

And the prices aren't US Gulf Coast — they're Singapore-benchmarked. Diesel is $3/bbl cheaper in Singapore. Fuel oil is $10/bbl more expensive. Every product needs a regional adjustment.

Failure #2 — Missing price series
EIA has no naphtha price series. No fuel oil series for Singapore. Had to estimate: naphtha at 90% of petrol, fuel oil at 65% of crude. These are approximations. The GRM was showing $13.49/bbl — realistic for an Indian complex refinery, but the uncertainty in the inputs was uncomfortable.

Chapter 4: The PPAC Wall

When government data fights back

India's Petroleum Planning & Analysis Cell (PPAC) publishes monthly reports with refinery throughput, utilization rates, and trade flows. Perfect data source. One problem: the PDFs are image-based. Not text. Not OCR-friendly. Just scanned tables.

Failure #3 — PDF extraction dead end
pdfplumber couldn't extract anything meaningful from the image-based tables. Built a Claude API PDF extractor that could read the images — but it needed API credits. Meanwhile, IOCL's website (for retail prices) used Sucuri WAF that blocked every scraping attempt. UN Comtrade for trade data required paid registration. Three data sources, three walls.
Pivot
Shipped with sample data for refinery throughput and trade flows. Not ideal, but the dashboard was usable. The real data pipeline was ready — just needed the API credits to unlock it. Sometimes shipping beats perfection.

Chapter 5: News Intelligence

Teaching the system to read

Built a news aggregator pulling from Google News (24 search queries) and 9 direct RSS feeds: Reuters, Al Jazeera, Bloomberg, Rigzone, S&P Global, Hellenic Shipping, Middle East Eye. Hundreds of articles flowing in every few hours.

The impact tagger used a 250+ keyword taxonomy across 10 categories with negation detection. "Oil prices fell" is bearish. "Oil prices did not fall" isn't. Getting this right required handling context per text segment, not just keyword matching.

Chapter 6: The Big Pivot — Scenario Engine

From dashboard to intelligence platform

The dashboard was good. But it was passive — showing data, not generating insight. The prompt that changed everything: "Can we add geopolitical scenario analysis? Like, what happens to Indian refiners if Hormuz closes?"

This turned a data dashboard into an intelligence platform. Five scenarios emerged, each grounded in analyst consensus from Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, Citi, Wood Mackenzie, and Rystad Energy:

Scenario 1m 3m 6m What it means
Managed Escalation $100 $97 $95 Tit-for-tat strikes, limited Hormuz disruption
Prolonged Standoff $92 $90 $85 Neither war nor peace, elevated premiums
Conflagration $140 $130 $120 Full regional war, Hormuz closed
Ceasefire & Talks $72 $68 $63 Formal ceasefire, markets normalize
Regime Change $88 $85 $80 Internal power shift, unpredictable transition

The key innovation: each scenario has preconditions (things that must be true for it to hold) and invalidators (events that would eliminate it). This gave the LLM structured criteria instead of open-ended vibes. Huge difference in output quality.

Chapter 7: Teaching an LLM to Score News

The scoring algorithm

Claude Haiku 4.5 reads every news article and scores it against all 5 scenarios on a [-1.0, +1.0] scale. Confirms a precondition? +0.3 to +0.6. Matches an invalidator? -0.7 to -1.0. Neutral? Near zero.

The signals feed into a softmax function (temperature=0.5) that converts average scores into probabilities summing to 100%. Then probability-weighted expected values for Brent crude and GRM across three horizons: 1-month, 3-month, 6-month.

Failure #4 — LLM tone control
Early narratives were dramatic. "Forces Indian refiners to declare force majeure." "Catastrophic supply chain collapse imminent." The LLM was writing thriller fiction, not intelligence briefs. Had to add explicit guardrails: no "force majeure", no "catastrophic", no "collapse", no "existential", no "unprecedented". State facts and mechanisms only. McKinsey tone, not CNN breaking news.
Breakthrough
Prompted the narrative generator as a "McKinsey senior partner" — ultra-concise, 3 bullets max, 15 words per bullet, no hedging. The output quality jumped dramatically. Every word had to earn its place.

Chapter 8: The Deployment Puzzle

Making it live without a server

The Streamlit app worked locally but needed a server to run. Deployed to Streamlit Community Cloud first, then Render.com. Both had cold start issues and the free tiers were slow.

Pivot — Go static
The insight: the dashboard only updates every few hours. Why run a server 24/7? Export the scenario analysis as a standalone HTML file. Deploy to GitHub Pages. Free, fast, globally cached CDN. The "backend" only runs during GitHub Actions — no server to maintain.

Built export_html.py — 800+ lines that read the database, compute EVs, render Mermaid diagrams, and output a responsive dark-mode HTML report. Added a refresh button that triggers via a Cloudflare Worker proxy (to hide the GitHub token), which dispatches a GitHub Actions run. The page auto-polls for updates and reloads when new content is detected.

Chapter 9: The Accuracy Question

Can you trust the numbers?

The system produces expected values, not forecasts. But the question kept nagging: are the probabilities any good?

Built an accuracy tracking system. Every refresh, the current prediction is snapshotted. When actual prices come in, the error is computed. A 10-day rolling average shows how far off the EV was. The daily email includes this — radical transparency about the system's limitations.

Ongoing tension
The softmax temperature is a magic number. Too high (T=3) and all scenarios get ~20%. Too low (T=0.1) and one scenario dominates at 90%+. Settled on T=0.5 as a balance between decisiveness and humility. But it's a judgment call, not a derivation.

Chapter 10: The Current State

What's running right now

Every 3 hours, GitHub Actions fires:

  1. 9 parallel scrapers pull data from EIA, yfinance, OilPrice, ExchangeRate, Google News, 9 RSS feeds, PPAC, OPEC, and company press releases
  2. 1,000+ articles are scored against 5 scenarios by Claude Haiku 4.5
  3. Softmax converts signals to probabilities across 3 horizons (1m, 3m, 6m)
  4. LLM generates strategic narratives and per-scenario assessments
  5. Static HTML is exported and deployed to GitHub Pages
  6. Daily email digest sent with probability shifts and top signals

The whole pipeline runs in ~90 seconds. No server. No infra costs beyond the Anthropic API.

What I Learned

  • Vibe coding works for ambitious projects — but you need domain intuition. The AI writes the code; you need to know what to ask for and when the output smells wrong.
  • Ship with sample data — don't let missing data sources block the whole project. The PPAC PDFs are still image-based, but the dashboard shipped anyway.
  • LLMs need guardrails — left unconstrained, Claude wrote thriller fiction. Explicit tone controls and word bans transformed the output.
  • Static > dynamic when updates are periodic — GitHub Pages with CI/CD is simpler and faster than maintaining a server for data that refreshes every 3 hours.
  • Preconditions + invalidators > open-ended prompts — giving the LLM structured scoring criteria massively improved signal quality.
  • Show your uncertainty — the accuracy tracker and the EV framing (not "forecast") keep the system honest.

Scoring Algorithm

1. LLM scores each article against 5 scenarios: signal in [-1.0, +1.0]
2. score[s] = AVG(signal) for scenario s across all articles
3. weight[s] = exp(score[s] / T) / Σ exp(score[i] / T)    [T = 0.5]
4. EV(Brent) = Σ weight[s] × scenario_price[s]
5. EV(GRM) = Σ weight[s] × scenario_grm[s]

Indian Refinery GRM

Indian Basket = 0.72 × Dubai/Oman + 0.28 × Brent

GRM = (0.42×Diesel + 0.22×Petrol + 0.12×Naphtha + 0.10×ATF + 0.08×FuelOil + 0.06×LPG) − Basket

Singapore adjustments: Diesel −$3, Petrol −$2.5, ATF −$2, FuelOil +$10, LPG +$5
Estimates: Naphtha = 90% of Petrol, Fuel Oil = 65% of Crude
